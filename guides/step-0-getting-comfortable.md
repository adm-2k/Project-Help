# Step 0: Getting Comfortable

> Before Step 1, a moment to settle in. If you're nervous about building software, read this first. It's short, and it's for you.

## You belong here

You do not need a coding background to do this. That isn't a polite lie to make you feel better; it's how this playbook is built. The people on your team who have never written a line of code in their lives are exactly the people this was made for. If you can describe what you want in plain words, you can build software here.

So take a breath. You're not behind. You're at the start, with everyone else.

## What you actually do here

Here's the honest shape of the work: you describe what you want, the AI does the typing, and you steer and check.

You are the one who decides what the app should do, who it's for, and whether what came back is any good. The AI writes the actual code. Your job is to give it a clear plan, read what it produces, try it out, and say "yes, that's right" or "no, that's not what I meant." You're the director, not the typist. That's a real job, and it's the one that matters most.

## Asking questions is the job

When you don't understand something, asking about it is not a sign that you're falling behind. It's the work itself.

Every confident-looking person around you was confused by `branch` and `merge` and `pull request` at some point. The ones who got comfortable are simply the ones who asked. A question you ask out loud saves the whole team the time of tripping over the same thing later. Treat your own confusion as useful information, not embarrassment.

## The AI is a patient tutor

Your AI builder isn't only there to write code. It's also the most patient teacher you'll ever have, and it will never sigh at you for asking the same thing twice. When a word or an idea doesn't make sense, just ask it, in plain English.

For example:

```
I'm new to this. Explain what a "pull request" is like I've never
heard the term, using a simple everyday comparison.
```

```
What does this error message mean, in plain English? What probably
caused it, and what's the safest thing for me to try first?
```

Ask it to slow down. Ask it to use an analogy. Ask it to assume you know nothing. It will, every time.

## How to find your way around

You don't have to hold the whole playbook in your head. A few places will orient you whenever you're lost:

- The [README](../README.md) is the map. It shows the whole loop, tells you where you are, and points you to the right step for whatever your team is doing right now.
- The [Glossary](../GLOSSARY.md) is your plain-English dictionary. Any word that looks like jargon, `commit`, `branch`, `repo`, `deploy`, is defined there in one or two friendly sentences.
- [GUIDE-ME](../GUIDE-ME.md) is the shortcut for when you would rather just ask. Paste it into ChatGPT or Claude, and the AI reads the playbook and tells you what to do next, in plain English.

When in doubt, open the README and ask, "where am I, and what do I do now?" It's built to answer exactly that.

## Your first win

Early on, your team is going to put a tiny page online, something small and real, so you can feel what shipping actually feels like before the stakes get higher. Watching something you helped make appear at a real web address, one you can open on your phone, is the moment "building software" stops being scary and starts being fun.

Your mentor will run this first one with you, so you don't have to worry about the setup. You'll see the whole arc, from idea to a live link, in miniature. After that, the rest of the playbook is just the same arc, bigger.
